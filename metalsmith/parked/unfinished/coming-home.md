`tomo-mi`

Your house, otherwise known as home, is its own entity. Home is part of us but not inside of us or connected to us like our body. However, home is also more than the loose objects that we call our possessions.

Home is an extension of ourselves. We leave it in order to return to it at a later time. We invite guests, but are essentially also guest in it. It supplies us in our primary need for shelter and safety and it is a basecamp for exploration. It is also a place for rest and being self-absorbed.

![coming-home](/img/topic-home.png)

We should treat home respectfully. The first point of contact is entering the home. Know that you enter the home with a specific feeling. Maybe you are tired and you want to lie down immediately. Or, you enter with loads of groceries and you need to fix dinner for your family. Or maybe you are just in a bad mood or incidentally in a very cheery mood.

Home has a state on its own; a feeling and a need, which requires a set of rituals on its own.

Homecoming is a meeting. It is an interaction, which consists of a feeling, a wish and an expectation. The first step in the ritual of homecoming is to become aware of the three aforementioned elements.

For example: "I am tired and overwhelmed after a long day and I wish to be in bed or on the sofa for a while to recover; I expect to enter a quiet and stimilus free environment."

If you live alone, you should be reasonably aware about how you left it and if it will meet your expectations. If you have a family or roommates it is trickier. Maybe your partner left the house last but in a hurry and left the home in a mess; or the kids are home before you are and they have organized a karaoke-safari in your home. Just as meeting a person you will have to deal with this.

The second step after entering the home mindfully is to experience and verbalize that home itself. You can thank it for the peace and quiet and the warm welcome of the flowres on the table; but also name that unrest and chaos and thank it nonetheless for welcoming you.

The third step is the transition ritual in which you pass from the outside world into the inside world. First, take everything into the entrance hall before proceeding. Do not storm in dropping bags left and right in every room you pass, even if they are the heavy groceries. Let the home get used to you first and you to it. Perform an act of recognition, this can be a bow, taking off your shoes or something else.

Then, you fully unpack everything and stow it away. This goes for groceries: so do n ot just unpack what you need to fix dinner in a hurry and leave the rest in a bag somewhere. This is disrespectful to the home that provides you for a proper place for everything you bring. It also goes for your backpack you take to and from work, even if you take the same stuff every day and you might just as well leave them in the bag for tomorrow. If you just drop your bag into a corner and leave everything inside, you are not only indicate that you do not trust the home with your stuff, but also that you would like to leave the home as quickly as possible. The last also goes for attention demanding possessions such as you smartphone. Leave them to charge in a cupboard or whatever, just don't keep it on your body.

After performing a small task for the home, such as a small cleanup or tidy-up you can expect something back from the home and spend attention to what the home has to offer without demanding more than what the home has to offer.
