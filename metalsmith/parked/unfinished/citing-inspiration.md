Sometimes building a framework of thoughts requires a lull in education and inspiration. This is especially so for non-scientific matters. In science it is central to make small progress on a heavy body of existing work and so you need full knowledge.

In all other cases inspiration may still be welcome, but really novel ideas you will not form because most ideas in the universe have been thought of already. What you can do instead is form your own collected image of existing ideas, and there is nothing wrong with that except that you skip a lot of searching and thinking. You also skip the continuous feedback with your own feelings in the process of coming up with the ideas if you simply latch on to someone else's story.

If you keep citing the ideas of others when you are creating your own framework of thoughts then several things go wrong.

A. You may be tempted to fall for the 'appeal to authority' fallacy.
B. You apply the citation in a context or domain that the original author did not intend of using it (which is very likely to happen since you are telling your own story)
C. It's about the idea, stupid, not the person who also thought about it.

Name dropping or citing purely as inspiration is fine and commendable. You acknowledge your are saying nothing new per se but still give it your own twist, which makes you responsible for the thing being said and not the person you are citing as inspiration.
